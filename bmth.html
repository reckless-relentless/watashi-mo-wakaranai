BRING ME THE HORIZON


Bring Me the Horizon is a British metal band that formed in 2004 in Sheffield, England, and over the years they’ve built a reputation for constantly changing and redefining their sound. Fronted by Oliver Sykes, along with guitarist Lee Malia, bassist Matt Kean, and drummer Matt Nicholls, the band has grown from a heavy underground act into one of the biggest names in modern rock.

They started out with a much heavier, chaotic style, which you can hear clearly on their debut album Count Your Blessings. It was loud, aggressive, and very much rooted in deathcore. Not long after, they began shifting their sound with Suicide Season, where things felt more structured and emotionally driven, while still keeping that intensity. By the time they released There Is a Hell Believe Me I've Seen It. There Is a Heaven Let's Keep It a Secret, it was clear they were experimenting more, bringing in atmospheric elements, layered production, and darker, more introspective themes.

A major turning point for the band came with Sempiternal, which introduced a more melodic and polished sound. This album really pushed them into the mainstream and is often seen as the moment everything changed for them. They continued in that direction with That's the Spirit, leaning further into alternative rock with bigger hooks and a more accessible style. Then came amo, where they took things even further by mixing in electronic, pop, and experimental influences, showing they weren’t afraid to completely step outside their original genre.

Alongside these albums, they also explored new ideas with Post Human: Survival Horror, a project that blends heavy music with modern production and a more conceptual approach. A lot of their newer sound was shaped by Jordan Fish, whose work with electronics and production added a whole new dimension to their music.

What makes Bring Me the Horizon stand out is how they’ve never stayed in one place for too long. Their music has gone from heavy and aggressive to melodic, experimental, and even pop-influenced at times, but it still feels like them. Combined with lyrics that often deal with mental health, personal struggles, and modern life, they’ve managed to connect with a huge global audience while continuing to evolve with every release.
